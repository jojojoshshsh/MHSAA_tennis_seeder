How the rankings work

Overview

These rankings aim to estimate the seed a player would receive at the MHSAA state tournament based on the full body of match results available on TennisReporting.com. The system is built around the idea that who you beat matters more than how many you beat — a win over a player who in turn beat many others carries more weight than a win over someone with no competitive record.

Rankings are computed separately for Singles and Doubles, across all four MHSAA divisions and all four flights. The algorithm runs in two layers: a graph-based seed ordering pipeline that determines the final ranked order, and a composite display score (TGRS) that gives each player a single at-a-glance number.

Role of TGRS in the pipeline: TGRS is primarily a display score, but it also serves as the last-resort tiebreaker in both the initial seed order (Step 2) and the adjacent fix-up pass (Step 4), after head-to-head, common opponents, and transitive dominance have all been exhausted. Because TGRS incorporates TrueSkill, reachability, strength of schedule, quality wins, and win percentage, it is a more complete proxy for overall quality than any single metric alone.

The Ranking Pipeline

Each gender + match type + flight combination is ranked as one cross-division group, then split into divisions afterward. The pipeline runs four steps.

1 Cycle Removal

Match results are loaded into a directed win graph: an arrow points from winner to loser. Before any ranking can happen, every cycle in this graph must be broken — a cycle means player A beat B, B beat C, and C beat A, which makes a strict ordering impossible.

The engine finds every cycle (of any length, including simple head-to-head upsets) and removes the oldest match on each cycle. This preserves the most recent competitive evidence and discards results that have been superseded by more recent play. This process repeats until the graph is fully acyclic (a DAG).

Matches that ended after only one set, or with a score of exactly "2-0 2-0" (a short-set placeholder), are excluded from the ranking graph entirely — they still count toward win/loss records but are not used for ordering.

2 Transitive Closure & Initial Order

On the acyclic graph, each player's transitive win set is computed: every player they can reach by following any chain of win-edges. If you beat A and A beat B, your transitive win set includes both A and B.

Players are sorted by the size of this set, descending. Beating players who have themselves beaten many others produces a larger reachable set and a higher seed. When two players' reachable sets are the same size (because they're incomparable in the graph), ties fall back in this order:

  1. More recent match activity (recency-weighted win/loss score)
  2. Better average score margin across all matches
  3. Higher TGRS composite score (see formula below)
  4. A fixed random tiebreak (for full determinism within a run)

3 Division Split

The cross-division order from Step 2 is partitioned into the four MHSAA divisions, preserving the relative order within each division. No new comparisons are made at this stage — the split is a pure filter on an already-settled order.

4 Adjacent Fix-Up Pass

Within each division, the engine walks adjacent pairs top-to-bottom and checks whether the lower-ranked player should actually outrank the one above them. Four criteria are checked in strict priority order:

PriorityCriterionHow it's decided
1 Head-to-head Most recent match between the two players
2 Common opponents — wins Who won more matches against shared opponents
3 Common opponents — margin If tied on wins: cumulative score margin against shared opponents
4 Transitive dominance Does one player's transitive win set contain the other?
5 TGRS composite score Higher raw TGRS (TrueSkill + reachability + SoS + quality wins + win %) as a last resort

If the lower-ranked player wins any of these checks, the two are swapped. The pass repeats from the top until a full pass produces zero swaps (the order is fully stable). The "Why ranked below" column shown on the site reflects whichever criterion decided each adjacent pair in the final stable order.

TrueSkill Rating

TrueSkill (Herbrich et al., 2007) is a Bayesian skill rating system originally developed at Microsoft Research. Each player is modelled as a Gaussian: μ (mu) is the estimated skill mean and σ (sigma) is the uncertainty. After every match both players' ratings are updated — the winner's μ rises and the loser's μ falls, with larger updates when the outcome was surprising given the current ratings.

σ starts high and shrinks as more matches are played. The conservative rating used in the TGRS formula is μ − 3σ, which is a lower-confidence bound — players with few matches are penalised for uncertainty rather than rewarded for it.

TrueSkill ratings are computed from the ranking-eligible match set (the same matches used in the win graph, after excluding single-set and "2-0 2-0" scores). They feed directly into the TGRS composite score, which in turn acts as the last-resort tiebreaker in the ranking pipeline.

TGRS — Composite Score & Last-Resort Tiebreaker

TGRS ("Group Ranking Score") is a composite number that serves two roles: it is the last-resort tiebreaker in both the initial seed order and the adjacent fix-up pass, and it is also the display score shown on the site so readers have a single sortable number per player.

The formula is:

TGRS = (2.0 × reachability) + (5.0 × ts_conservative) + (0.5 × sos) + (1.5 × quality_wins) + (1.0 × win_pct)
ComponentWeightWhat it measures
reachability 2.0 Number of players reachable through chains of wins in the cross-division match graph
ts_conservative 5.0 TrueSkill conservative rating (μ − 3σ) from the full cross-division model
sos 0.5 Average TrueSkill μ of all opponents faced (strength of schedule)
quality_wins 1.5 Count of wins against opponents whose TrueSkill μ is at or above the starting value (25.0) — i.e. "at least average" opponents
win_pct 1.0 Wins ÷ total matches played in this group

TGRS Scaled

The raw TGRS is min-max scaled to a 0–100 band within each division+flight group, so the top player scores 100 and the bottom player scores 0. This makes it easy to compare players within the same bracket without worrying about the absolute numbers. The scaled version is shown on the site; the raw version is what the ranking pipeline uses internally as a tiebreaker.

Note: Because TGRS is computed after the transitive closure is built (but before the adjacent fix-up pass runs), it incorporates reachability from the same DAG used everywhere else in the pipeline — so it is genuinely consistent with, not independent from, the graph-based ranking logic.

Eligibility & Filtering

Before any player enters the ranking pipeline, two filters are applied:

  • Minimum matches: a player must have at least 5 recorded matches in a given gender + match type + flight group to appear in rankings for that group. This is counted from all matches including excluded scores, so a player is not unfairly dropped just because some of their results were single-set or forfeit-style.
  • Per-school deduplication: if a school has two players surviving the match minimum in the same flight slot, only the one with the most recent match date is kept. The other's matches are removed entirely to avoid contaminating head-to-head and common-opponent data.

The following match scores are excluded from all ranking calculations (but still counted in win/loss records):

  • Matches that ended after only one set (e.g. a retirement mid-match)
  • Matches with a score of exactly "2-0 2-0" (a short-set placeholder used for walkovers)

Data & Limitations

Match data is crawled automatically from TennisReporting.com, the platform used by most Michigan high school tennis programs to report results. Rankings are rebuilt and republished daily at 4am EDT.

Known limitations:

  • The minimum match requirement means players early in the season or with few recorded matches may not appear.
  • Division assignments come from TennisReporting school metadata. Schools with unusual or missing labels are normalised where possible; some may land in Division 4 when they shouldn't. Corrections can be submitted via correct_divisions.csv in the GitHub repository.
  • Doubles pairs are treated as single entities. Different pair combinations from the same school are ranked separately.
  • Regular season and tournament bracket matches are both included. Where deduplication is possible it is applied, but some double-reporting may remain.
  • Rankings cover a single season only — no carry-over from prior years.
  • Because the ranking order is determined by graph structure and pairwise comparisons rather than a single score, adjacent players near the boundary of any seed bracket may legitimately swap from day to day as new match data arrives.
